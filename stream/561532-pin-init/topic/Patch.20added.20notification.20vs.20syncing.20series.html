Topic: Patch added notification vs syncing series

Gary Guo (Jul 10 2026 at 12:20):

Currently our workflow is that commits are added to GitHub, and then we create syncing series to synchronize them to pin-init-next. But these series typically got no reviews at all, and leaving it on the list untaken reduces testing time in linux-next.

I wonder if the workflow can be changed similar to how stable tree is managed, where the patch is applied directly to pin-init-next, and then a email of "patch xxx is added to pin-init-next" is sent to the list? If something is broken then it could be taken out still or fixed by a follow-up commit.

For bigger features or things that touch files outside pin-init we can still fallback to the syncing series workflow.

Thoughts @Miguel Ojeda @Benno Lossin ?

Miguel Ojeda (Jul 10 2026 at 13:46):

So the standard rules are that the patches need to have been posted to a mailing list etc. There are times that I also feel the same, i.e. that it would save me time to just apply some cleanups directly, but I still go through the list to be "proper" and to give a chance to people to give tags etc.

Regarding time in linux-next, if they are small series, then I don't think it matters much, and you could always put them tentatively in linux-next and rebase later if needed.

i.e. that would give you the best of both worlds: standard process, and yet good testing times.

But if it would simplify your life significantly, perhaps Mark is willing to make an exception for you since it is a vendored thing and you are the upstream maintainer (unlike e.g. zerocopy). But we would need to then explain it to Linus too and so on. Not sure if it is worth it, and Mark may fear others may ask for similar exceptions as well and it snowballs...
