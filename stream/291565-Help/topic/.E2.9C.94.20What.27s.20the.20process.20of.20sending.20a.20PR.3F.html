No worries! People may be just busy with other things and not have the time to look into your PRs.

From my own experience, you may want to be a little more "async" on the open source work, people usually have different schedules and priorities, so sometimes we have to wait. Maybe you can take your time to work on something else in the project? Or you can polish your current PRs: for example, I saw you use "cpus_read_lock" in 728, could you make it a RWLock?

On the other hand, if you want to work on something for quick feedbacks, you can audit the existing code and see if there is any bug. Usually bug fixing is high prioritized (even better if you have the test cases), and even if you don't end up with any PR, you get to know the code better.

Of course, pinging the maintainers from time to time is OK ;-)

Boqun Feng (Aug 30 2022 at 16:47):

Also, if I understand correctly, this project is still a prototype. Miguel Ojeda (Aug 30 2022 at 18:03):

Boqun's advice is right. In particular, before the trimming down that happened in v9, we were very careful merging things into the rustbranch since we were sending most of it upstream as an example.

However, now we can take the better approach of sending things bit by bit, which means each piece will be submitted as part of the normal kernel workflow. Ideally, each subsystem would be maintained by their respective maintainers, and the rust-next branch will keep the "core Rust support" patches only.

In any case, to be clear, merging something into the rustbranch does not necessarilly mean that it will land upstream -- the branch is more like a "common playground" (or "integration branch" if you will) where we were putting a lot of things that were sufficiently reviewed etc., since this was an experiment and we wanted to have a common CI etc.
